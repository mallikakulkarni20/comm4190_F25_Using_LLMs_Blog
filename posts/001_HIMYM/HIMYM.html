The Show

If you have never watched “How I Met Your Mother”, stop immediately. Open Netflix, pay for a subscription, and watch the first episode.

Okay, welcome back! Life changing, right? Anyway, you just watched a sitcom script from writers in 2005 write iconic scenes to appeal to an audience in the past. My question is, what does that scene look like if you pummeled it much further in the past.

This is an experiment I’m calling “How I Met Your Modor” (think Old English friends hanging out at a bar in Manhattan). The goal is to see if an LLM can rewrite an iconic scene from How I Met Your Mother in starkly contrasted time periods and emotional states. Will it be able to preserve the emotional core, but now appeal to audiences that are relevant to the culture of society back then? Let’s shove Ted and Robin into the: * 5th century AD * Trojan War * 1920s * 2020s.

The Subject of the Experiment

Ted meets Robin! He makes an iconic mistake at the end of their first date: “I think I’m in love with you.”

I prompted GPT-5 with:

“Recall the scene in the first episode of How I Met Your Mother where Ted tells Robin he loves her. First produce the base case, then rewrite the scene as if you were a writer in the following eras, by catering to relevant audiences and representing the tone of the era: * 5th century AD * Trojan War * 1920s * 2020s (Gen Z)”

Here’s the lab report.

Base Case

Here’s the base case: the original script.

Robin and Ted are dancing in Robin’s apartment. All five dogs are watching from the couch.

Robin: I think I like your Olive Theory.

Ted: I think I like your French Horn.

Robin: I think I like your nose.

Ted: I think I’m in love with you.

[Cut to the bar]

Lily, Marshall, Barney (in unison): What?!

[Cut to 2030]

Son and Daughter: What?!

[Cut to Robin’s Apartment again]

Robin: What?

Ted: So, Orlando. You gonna hit Disney World?

Robin: You love me?

Ted: Oh God, I can’t believe I said that. Why did I say that? Who says that? I should just go. (Gets up)

Robin: Hold on. (Hands him olives) Promised you these.

Ted: Olives. Thanks. I love you. What’s wrong with me?

The Verdict

Each version loses impact. By simply changing the meaning of words, the LLM was not able to capture the true sentiment and feel of the script. This makes me believe even more that chatbots are purely pattern matchers and cannot generate creative information that is not just a slight alteration from the base case.

Final Takeaways

Can LLMs match tone and subtext?

LLMs can match tone and subtext, but they do not understand the complexities of language and pop culture over time. The originality of a time period is not easily matched. Instead of adapting an iconic plot to a time period, the LLM adapted specific phrases and words to a time period.

If I had prompted the LLM and emphasized creativity, then perhaps it would have wandered more with the script and created something that the different audiences would actually like.
