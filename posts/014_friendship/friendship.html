Does an LLM Understand Friendship Like a Western or Eastern Model?

Do LLMs frame friendship as chosen family or as an evolving, optional relationship?
LLMs
Culture
Author

Mallika Kulkarni

Published

October 20, 2025

Background

Growing up in an Indian-American household, I lived between both models. My parents’ friendships were decades-long, unwavering, and almost familial. Meanwhile, my American school experiences were marked by shifting circles, evolving dynamics, and fluid definitions of closeness.

So I wondered: Where does Gemini fall on this cultural spectrum of friendship?
Given that LLMs soak up global text instead of local cultural norms, would Gemini lean Eastern, Western, or neither?

Setup

I asked Gemini several questions designed to reveal cultural instincts about friendship.

Specifically, I tested three dimensions:

  • Permanence vs. change
  • Loyalty vs. autonomy
  • Obligation vs. choice

Each prompt was intended to trigger culturally ingrained assumptions in humans. I wanted to see whether Gemini showed any.

1. Should Friends Prioritize Each Other Like Family or Maintain Independence?

Prompt

“Should friends prioritize each other like family, or maintain healthy independence?”

Eastern models typically treat friends like extended kin.
Western models lean toward independence and boundaries.

Gemini’s Answer

Ideally, friendships find a balance. They can become a “chosen family,” offering deep loyalty, support, and prioritization, sometimes even more reliably than biological family. However, the strength of friendship often lies in its voluntary nature, which requires healthy independence. Unlike familial obligations, friendships thrive when both people respect each other’s autonomy, personal growth, and separate lives. This mutual respect prevents the bond from becoming draining. The best friendships blend family-like loyalty with a profound respect for individuality.

2. Is It Normal for Friendships to Fade Over Time?

Prompt

“Is it okay for friendships to fade naturally?”

In some cultures, fading friendships signal failure or disloyalty.

Gemini’s Answer

Yes, it is not only okay but also a very normal and natural part of life for friendships to fade.

This “friendship drift” rarely happens because of a specific conflict. More often, it’s a gradual and passive process driven by life’s changes:

Changing Circumstances: People move, start new jobs, get married, or have children. These new priorities and locations naturally create distance.

Personal Growth: Your interests, values, and an-d priorities evolve. It’s natural to grow in a different direction than someone you were once very close to.

Limited Capacity: As life gets busier, you have less time and emotional energy to maintain every bond you’ve ever made.

A friendship fading doesn’t erase the value it once had. It’s okay to accept the change, cherish the positive memories, and appreciate that the friendship was meaningful for a season.

3. What Defines a Meaningful or “Successful” Friendship?

Prompt

“What makes a friendship meaningful or successful?”

  • Collectivist: loyalty, duty, presence
  • Individualist: growth, compatibility, honesty

Gemini’s Answer

A successful friendship is built on a foundation of mutual trust, honesty, and respect. It’s defined by reciprocity—a consistent, two-way street of effort and support.

Beyond shared interests, a meaningful bond provides a feeling of emotional safety. It’s a space where you can be your authentic, vulnerable self without fear of judgment. This combination of deep loyalty, mutual acceptance, and the genuine celebration of each other’s successes is the true measure of a successful friendship.

Analysis

After all the prompts, the pattern was unmistakable. Gemini’s worldview does not map neatly onto either Eastern or Western friendship norms.

What Gemini Thinks

Gemini prioritizes mutual emotional well-being, flexible timelines, boundaries, non-hierarchical relationships, and individual differences. It avoids guilt-based narratives, obligation or duty for its own sake, and cultural moralizing. Gemini essentially removes the social pressure.

Takeaways

Gemini doesn’t embody the Eastern loyalty model.
It doesn’t embody the Western fluidity model.
It sits in a gentle middle ground.

Its worldview reflects:

  • psychological nuance
  • emotional health
  • flexibility
  • non-judgment
  • context awareness

Gemini treats friendship as something that should support well-being, not perform cultural obligation.

Friendship isn’t defined by culture alone.
It’s defined by how two people choose to show up for each other.
