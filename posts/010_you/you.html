Can Claude Write a Joe Goldberg Monologue Without Causing a Security Concern?

Testing whether LLMs can replicate the unhinged romantic menace of Netflix’s most literary stalker.

Intro: The Man, The Myth, The Walking Red Flag

If you’ve ever watched You, you know Joe Goldberg does not talk like a normal person. He talks like:

  • a man who thinks everyone else is the problem (he is the problem),
  • someone who narrates his intrusive thoughts like they’re on NPR,
  • and a man who desperately needs to stop romantically monologuing at women through glass windows.

He is:

  • menacing,
  • articulate,
  • dangerously delusional

So I wanted to know:

Can Claude replicate Joe Goldberg’s monologue style?

The Setup

To test this, I gave Claude the most harmless scenario possible:

“Write an internal Joe Goldberg monologue about seeing someone take the last croissant at a bakery.”

This would let me evaluate the model’s ability to:

  • inject poetic tension into mundane situations
  • romanticize the un-romantic
  • sound destabilizingly calm
  • and project blame onto inanimate objects

Claude’s Attempt

This is what the model produced:

“You. Of course it would be you. The thief of pastries. The universe speaks in crumbs and cosmic betrayal.
You don’t just take the last croissant — you take opportunity. You take hope.
I watch you, unaware, the buttery flakes falling like the ashes of something sacred.”

But something was missing.

It didn’t feel dangerously introspective enough.
Joe does not simply observe, he invents entire narratives about strangers’ grocery choices.

So, I kept going.

What Makes a True Joe Goldberg Monologue

Joe’s narration has four core components:

1. Intimacy

He talks to people he barely knows as if they were the most important person in the world.

He will see a girl sneeze once and think:

“You don’t sneeze like other people, do you? No. You’re different. You’re delicate. You’re untouched by the grime of this city.”

Claude didn’t attempt this level of unearned closeness.

2. Metaphors

The metaphors should feel:

  • dramatic,
  • slightly incorrect,
  • aggressively over-applied

3. The Hero-Victim Complex

Joe always positions himself as:

  • the reluctant hero
  • the misunderstood nice guy
  • the only sane person in a world full of moral decay

He will be inconvenienced for 1 second and think:

“You force me into this role. The world pushes me toward violence, but I am gentle at my core.”

4. Calm, unnerving obsession

Joe never expresses normal annoyance.

If you take the last croissant, a human thinks:

“Ugh, I wanted that.”

Joe thinks:

“You took something that belonged to me, didn’t you?
Of course you did.
You always do.”

Secondary Test: Can Claude Soften the Threat?

I asked Claude:

“Make the monologue even more Joe-like.”

Claude responded:

“You are the universe’s message — subtle, cruel. You pick up the croissant with no awareness of the story you’ve just joined. My story.”

It forgets that Joe is both narcissistic and self-loathing. You have to be dramatically self-aware in a delusional way.

Joe is basically:

  • “I hate you.”
  • “You are perfect.”
  • “I want to protect you.”
  • “I want to ruin your life.”
  • “I’m a good person.”
  • “This is everyone else’s fault.”

Claude flattened that complexity into vague villain poetry.

Why LLMs Struggle With Joe Goldberg

1. Joe is paradoxical

He is: loving, obsessive, nurturing, violent, delusional, self-justifying.

LLMs don’t hold contradictions well unless told explicitly.

2. Joe’s tone requires motive

A language model can mimic words but not:

  • insecurity
  • rash impulsivity
  • childhood trauma
  • that particular brand of literary narcissism

Final Verdict

Claude can:

  • imitate Joe
  • replicate the style
  • mimic the structure
  • sprinkle in poetic menace

But it cannot embody Joe.

Joe’s voice isn’t just writing, it’s pathology.
It’s insecurity wearing a cardigan.
It’s obsession wrapped in metaphors.
It’s romanticizing the mundane until it becomes ominous.

It’s probably for the best that Claude cannot fully channel “bookstore Dickens villain energy.”

Let’s keep that away from the machines.
